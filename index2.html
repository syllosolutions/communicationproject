The Bargaining Table

Why do rational nations choose war when peace would leave both better off? Step into the shoes of a head of state and find out.

War is costly. So for almost any dispute, there exists a deal both sides prefer to fighting. That deal is the bargaining range. This game lets you find it — and discover the three reasons bargaining breaks down and war happens anyway.

The Idea

War is a failed bargain

Two states, Azuria and Crovia, both claim one strip of border territory worth 100 points. They can fight over it — or split it. Here is the key insight your professor built the whole war unit around:

If they go to war, the likely outcome is a "probable new border" — but both sides also pay the costs of fighting: blood, treasure, destruction. Subtract those costs and you get each side's true value of war.

The formula:   Each side's expected outcome − its costs of war = what it will accept. The gap that opens up between them is the bargaining range. Every deal inside the green zone leaves both sides better off than war. War is only "rational" if something stops them from reaching it.

So why does war ever happen? Your professor names three failures. You'll face each one as a round in the negotiation ahead:

1 · Incomplete Information

You can't see how tough the other side really is — and they have every reason to bluff.

2 · Commitment Problems

Even a good deal is worthless if you can't trust them to keep it tomorrow.

3 · Indivisible Goods

Some prizes seem impossible to split — a holy city, a capital — making it all-or-nothing.

The Bargaining Model

Why it matters now

The model on the world stage: Russia–Ukraine

Abstract sliders are one thing. Here is the same logic in the defining war of our time. Before February 2022, Russia and Ukraine had a bargaining range over NATO membership, Donbas autonomy, and language policy. A deal existed that both could have preferred to war. It failed — and all three mechanisms are why.

🕵️

Incomplete information

Putin badly misjudged three things: Ukrainian resolve, NATO's unity, and his own army's readiness. He expected Kyiv to fall in days. Had Russia known what was coming, the pre-war bargain would have looked very different — but each side had an incentive to misrepresent, so the truth only emerged on the battlefield.

🤝

Commitment problems

Russia violated the 1994 Budapest security assurances in 2014, so it can't credibly promise not to invade again after any ceasefire. That's exactly why Ukraine demands binding guarantees from a third-party "coalition of the willing" — to enforce a deal neither side can credibly keep alone.

🏛️

Indivisible goods

Both sides have constructed Crimea and the annexed regions as legally indivisible — Russia by constitution, Ukraine by a clause barring ceding land without a referendum. Yet functional fixes (a frozen frontline, de-facto control without legal cession) remain conceivable. As your professor stresses, "indivisibility" is often strategic, not real.

As of May 2026 Russia controls roughly 20% of Ukrainian territory, casualties number in the hundreds of thousands, and competing ceasefires hint at a return to the bargaining table. The model doesn't predict whether peace holds — but it tells us exactly what to watch.

The one idea to walk away with: war is a puzzle. Because fighting is costly, a peaceful split almost always exists that beats war for everyone. When war happens anyway, it's because of incomplete information, commitment problems, or indivisible goods — and each has a fix: credible signals, third-party enforcement, and creative division. That's not just theory. It's the checklist diplomats are using on Russia–Ukraine right now.
